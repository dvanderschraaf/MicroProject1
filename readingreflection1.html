Reading and Response: "The Design of Everyday Things" - 05/01/26

For my Micro-Project, over the different stages, I turned it into a survival based interaction where the user controls the mouse to get points by clicking circles while avoiding the square that follows the mouse. The system is controlled by an energy variable that influences the squares speed, size, behavior and state which creates a cycle of movement, exhaustion, collapse and recovery. After reading Norman's concepts it helped me realize that my interaction does work but is not always as clear as it could have been.

1. Affordances & Signifiers

The main real affordance in my system is the movement of the cursor and the ability to click on the circles. Clicking is how the player earns points and the movement is how the player avoids the square. The affordances are pretty visual because the circles' color makes them stand out, and the square is clear enough to see so you can avoid it. It would not be obvious if it was someone's first time playing as well as if there were no rules, rules weren't clear enough, or they just skipped over the rules entirely. There is no explicit instruction besides the written text above the actual sketch. In the case that the sketch was the only thing presented, the player would have to play over and over again to figure out what to do. This showed me that affordances are momentarily hidden and revealed after interaction which Norman would probably critique.

2. Feedback

Feedback in the system is present but it is uneven. The immediate feedback from the circle is clicked to increase the score, and then a new circle is spawned after, which is clear and effective. The square's movement is providing continuous feedback about its state, specifically when it slows down because the energy is low. The color shift in the background from red to green helps communicate the energy levels. There are less clear moments of feedback like when the rectangle collapses. It stops moving but the reason for this pause isn't really that clear and understandable for a new player. The pause before recovery may be confusing to a new user rather than an intentional part of the system. Norman would say something along the lines about how the internal state logic may not be presentable enough in the actual experience, like the transitions between collapse and recovery.

3. Mapping

I feel mapping in the system is mostly based off of instincts. The mouse controls position and clicking on the circles gives you points. I think this is a pretty rational and familiar interaction pattern. The relationship between increasing your points and the difficulty of avoiding the square is also logical but not very visually communicated. The player might feel the game getting harder as they get more points but not understand the correlation between getting points and the square getting faster. The confusion is not intentional but in my opinion, contributes to the unpredictability which I feel enhances the gameplay and the experience. It does weaken clarity through the design perspective though.

4. Constraints

The constraints I have in my system include the limited number of circles, the energy system and the game over condition. These constraints guide the behavior by forcing the player to be precise with the mouse and move quickly, while avoiding the square. The energy system is an invisible constraint that limits the difficulty, by controlling the variables of the square. A lot of these constraints aren't that visible, for example, the energy threshold for the states of movement is only communicated through visual cues, the titles. These constraints shape the game but can make things difficult if the player doesn't understand how the system works.

5. The Biggest Design Weakness

My biggest design weakness in my project is the lack of clear communication about the system states and its rules. The system is somewhat structurally complex, though that complexity isn't always visible to the player. Norman would tell me that the Gulf of Evaluation is to open in moments where the player may not be able to interpret what is happening, such as the collapsed state and the increase in difficulty. The funny thing is I had friends play and I saw this in real time. The system works but it relies too heavily on the player to figure things out on their own instead of helping them understand.

6. What is Your Biggest Takeaway from the Text?

I think that my biggest takeaway from the text is that interaction design is not just about creating behavior but about making things understandable through clear communication. LXD i see is about shaping the way people act but it only works when users can clearly understand what they are doing and what the responses will be. Every action comes with a reaction so making both as clear as possible is the most important thing. This reading was helpful because now I have more perspective and can review my work differently. It made me realize that clear communication is not only important in physical relationships lol, clear communication is how you make everything positive, make everything work, and make everything function properly.
